The G-test is a close relative to the $\chi^2$ test, in fact the $\chi^2$ test is an approximation of the G-test. Per Wikipedia:

The commonly used $\chi^2$ tests for goodness of fit to a distribution and for independence in contingency tables are in fact approximations of the log-likelihood ratio on which the G-tests are based. The general formula for Pearson's chi-squared test statistic is $ \chi^2 = \sum_i { \frac{ (O_i - E_{i} )^2 }{ E_i } } $. The approximation of G by chi squared is obtained by a second order Taylor expansion of the natural logarithm around 1. The general formula for G is $ G = 2 \sum_i { O_i \cdot \ln \frac{O_i}{E_i} } $, where $O_i$ is the observed count in a cell, $E_i$ is the expected count under the null hypothesis, $\ln$ denotes the natural logarithm, and the sum is taken over all non-empty cells.
